Executive Summary 
 
MedLens is a mobile application — available on iOS and Android — that translates complex 
medication information into clear, plain everyday language that anyone can understand. The 
app is built for patients, caregivers, and everyday people who are handed a prescription or 
medication leaflet and struggle to make sense of what it says. 
 
Medication leaflets and prescription documents are written in dense medical jargon designed for 
healthcare professionals, not for the patients who actually take the drugs. A patient who picks 
up a prescription and cannot understand the dosage instructions, warnings, or side effects is at 
genuine risk of taking their medication incorrectly. This is the daily experience of the majority of 
patients worldwide, and it is even more acute in regions where English is a second language. 
 
MedLens addresses this directly. A user opens the app, types the name of a medication, and 
the app retrieves official drug data from the OpenFDA API. That data is passed to DeepSeek AI, 
which rewrites the medical information in simple, everyday language — what the drug is for, 
how to take it, what warnings to be aware of, and what side effects are possible. Beyond the 
core explanation, users can save medications to a personal medicine cabinet, check for risky 
drug combinations in plain language, export a summary card to share with a caregiver, and 
activate an 'Explain Like I'm 12' toggle for even simpler language. 
 
The app runs on a fully free technology stack — OpenFDA for drug data, DeepSeek API for AI-
powered rewriting, and Supabase for user accounts and saved cabinets — built with React 
Native, the same framework that powers the ChatGPT mobile app. MedLens is not a diagnostic 
tool. It is a health literacy tool that helps people understand the medications they have already 
been prescribed, so they can take them correctly, safely, and with confidence. 
 
Problem Statement 
 
Medication leaflets and prescription information are written in dense medical jargon. Patients 
routinely misunderstand dosage instructions, side effects, and drug interactions — especially in 
regions where English is a second language. Key problems include: 
 
• Low Health Literacy: Many users cannot correctly interpret dosage instructions, 
warnings, or side effects from standard prescription labels or medication leaflets. 
• Medication Misuse: Misunderstanding instructions leads to incorrect usage, missed 
doses, or dangerous over-use — all of which carry serious health consequences. 
• Language Barriers: English-only medical information limits accessibility in multilingual 
regions, particularly across West Africa where English is a second language for the 
majority of the population. 
• Limited Accessibility: Users with lower literacy levels or visual impairments face 
additional barriers in reading and understanding written medication instructions.

Product Vision and Goals 
 
Vision 
To become a trusted mobile platform that helps people understand their medications clearly, 
safely, and confidently — in language that works for them. 
 
Core Goals 
• Clarity: Translate medical jargon into simple, human-friendly language anyone can 
understand. 
• Safety: Prevent harmful misinterpretation of medication instructions by providing 
accurate, plain-language explanations grounded in verified drug data. 
• Accessibility: Support voice playback and simplified language modes so no user is left 
behind, regardless of literacy level or language background. 
• Trust: Provide transparent, responsibly simplified information — always clearly sourced 
from official drug data, never invented by AI. 
• Empowerment: Enable users to make informed decisions about their medications and 
have better conversations with their healthcare providers. 
 
Target Users and Use Cases 
 
Primary Target Users 
• Adults managing their own prescriptions 
• Caregivers assisting elderly parents, children, or family members with medications 
• Individuals with moderate literacy challenges 
• Non-native English speakers in multilingual regions

Key Features (MVP) 
 
The Minimum Viable Product is defined by five core features, taken directly from the project 
brief. These are the committed features for the first public release of the MedLens mobile app. 
 
1. Drug Search with Autocomplete Suggestions 
Users can search for any medication by typing its name into the search bar. The app displays 
autocomplete suggestions as the user types, pulling from the OpenFDA Drug API in real time. 
The search supports both brand names and generic names, and handles common spelling 
variations. Once a medication is selected, the app retrieves the official drug label data and 
passes it to the DeepSeek AI layer, which rewrites the information in plain, everyday language 
and displays it as a structured summary card. 
 
2. "Explain Like I'm 12" Toggle

Every drug summary screen includes an 'Explain Like I'm 12' toggle. When activated, DeepSeek 
re-processes the current drug's information using an even simpler prompt — shorter sentences, 
no technical vocabulary, and everyday analogies where helpful. The toggle is clearly labelled so 
users understand it provides an additional simplification layer, not a different or reduced version 
of the medical facts. 
 
This feature is designed for users who find the standard plain-language summary still too 
complex, including elderly users, low-literacy users, and users reading in their second language. 
 
3. Personal Medicine Cabinet 
Users can save any medication they have searched to their personal medicine cabinet — a 
persistent list of their medications stored in their Supabase account. The medicine cabinet is 
accessible from the home screen and allows users to view the full plain-language summary of 
any saved medication at any time without re-searching. 
 
The cabinet stores the drug name and its simplified explanation. It does not track dosage 
schedules or make dosage recommendations in MVP. 
 
4. Interaction Checker 
Users can select multiple medications from their medicine cabinet and run an interaction check. 
The system displays interaction warnings only when reliable data is available from OpenFDA. If 
data is unavailable or inconclusive, the app clearly communicates this: 
 
"We cannot confirm interactions between these medications. Please consult a healthcare 
professional." 
 
Interaction results are displayed in plain language — not clinical severity labels. The checker 
does not guess or infer. It only surfaces interactions supported by verified data. 
 
5. Exportable Summary Card 
After viewing any drug summary, users can generate an exportable summary card. The card 
includes the drug name, what it does, how to take it, and key warnings — formatted cleanly for 
sharing. Users can share the card with a caregiver or family member directly from the app using 
the native iOS and Android share sheet (WhatsApp, email, or any installed messaging app).

Detailed User Flows 
 
Flow 1 — Onboarding & Authentication

1. User downloads and opens MedLens for the first time. Splash screen displays briefly 
before the onboarding screens load. 
2. Onboarding screens walk the user through what MedLens does — three simple slides: 
search a medication, get a plain-language explanation, save to your cabinet. 
3. After the final onboarding slide, a brief loading spinner plays and the user is taken 
directly to the home screen. No sign-up decision is required upfront. 
4. User is already in guest mode and can immediately start searching for a medication and 
viewing plain-language explanations. 
5. When the user attempts to save a medication to their cabinet, they are prompted to 
create a free account or log in. This is the only moment sign-up is introduced. 
6. Returning users who have already signed up bypass onboarding and go directly to the 
log-in screen. On successful login, they land on their home screen with their cabinet 
restored. 
 
Flow 2 — Medication Explanation 
1. User opens MedLens. Home screen displays a search bar and their medicine cabinet. 
2. User types a medication name. Autocomplete suggestions appear from OpenFDA as 
they type. 
3. User selects the medication from suggestions. 
4. App retrieves the official FDA label data from OpenFDA API. 
5. Backend passes the label data to DeepSeek API with a plain-language prompt. 
6. AI returns a structured plain-language summary: what it is, how to take it, warnings, side 
effects. 
7. User reads the summary. They can toggle 'Explain Like I'm 12' for a further simplified 
version. 
8. User taps 'Save to Cabinet' to add the medication to their personal medicine cabinet. 
 
Flow 3 — Medicine Cabinet & Interaction Check 
1. User opens their medicine cabinet from the home screen. 
2. Cabinet displays all saved medications with drug name and one-line summary. 
3. User taps 'Check Interactions.' They select two or more medications from the cabinet. 
4. App queries OpenFDA for known interaction data between the selected drugs. 
5. Results display as a plain-language flag: safe, use caution, or consult a professional. 
 
Flow 4 — Export & Share Summary Card 
1. User views a drug summary screen. 
2. User taps 'Export Summary.' App generates a formatted summary card. 
3. Card includes: drug name, what it does, how to take it, key warnings. 
4. User taps 'Share.' Native iOS/Android share sheet opens. 
5. User selects WhatsApp, email, or any installed app to send the card.

Technical Requirements 
 
Architecture Overview 
MedLens is a mobile application built with React Native — the same framework used to build 
the ChatGPT mobile app. React Native produces a fully native iOS and Android application from 
a single codebase, delivering the smooth screen transitions, native keyboard behaviour, and 
fluid scroll physics that define a professional mobile app experience. The app is distributed via 
the Apple App Store and Google Play Store. 
 
A branded splash screen — displaying the MedLens logo on a teal background — appears on 
every app launch using the @notifee/react-native and react-native-splash-screen libraries, 
configured natively for both platforms. 
 
The architecture separates concerns cleanly across four layers: the React Native mobile 
frontend, a lightweight Node.js/Express backend API, external data and AI services, and the 
Supabase database.

Security and Compliance 
• Data in Transit: All API communication encrypted over HTTPS/TLS. Supabase 
enforces SSL on all database connections. 
• Authentication: Supabase Auth manages JWT-based sessions. Supports 
email/password and Google OAuth sign-in. 
• Data Minimization: MedLens stores only the drug names users explicitly save. No 
sensitive medical records are stored beyond user inputs. No data is sold or shared with 
third parties. 
• AI Guardrails: DeepSeek is instructed via system prompt to only rewrite provided FDA 
label data — never to generate new medical instructions, guess missing information, or 
recommend treatments. If reliable data is unavailable, the app displays: 'We could not 
find reliable information for this medication. Please consult a healthcare professional.'

Metrics of Success 
 
Product Metrics 
• Number of Active Users (DAU/MAU): Daily and monthly active users — the primary 
indicator of adoption and sustained engagement. 
• Number of Searches per User: Average drug searches per active user per session — 
measures genuine utility. 
• Feature Usage: Usage rates for ELI12 toggle, voice playback, cabinet saves, interaction 
checker, and export card — identifies which features deliver the most value. 
• Onboarding Completion Rate: Percentage of sign-ups that complete account setup 
and run their first drug search. 
 
Trust Metrics 
• Explanation Helpfulness Rating: In-app 'Was this helpful? Yes / No' prompt shown 
after each summary. Target: above 80% positive. 
• Successful Explanation Rate: Percentage of searches that return a full plain-language 
summary vs. a 'not found' result. 
• Frequency of 'Data Not Found' Cases: Tracks coverage gaps in the OpenFDA 
database — informs the prioritisation of additional data sources. 
 
Accessibility Metrics 
• ELI12 Toggle Usage Rate: How often users activate the simplified mode — signals 
when standard plain language is still too complex. 
• Text-to-Speech Usage Rate: Percentage of users who activate voice playback — 
indicates the size of the low-literacy and visually impaired user segment. 
• App Store Rating: Target 4.5 / 5 stars or above on both the Apple App Store and 
Google Play Store within 3 months of launch. 
 
Risks and Limitations

Confidential — Capstone 2026     Page 11 
• AI Simplification Nuance: AI may occasionally miss nuance or simplify a warning in a 
way that reduces its urgency. Mitigated by restricting DeepSeek to only rewrite provided 
data and by displaying the FDA source alongside every summary. 
• OpenFDA Data Coverage: OpenFDA covers FDA-approved medications and does not 
include non-US drug brands. Mitigated in MVP by clearly communicating this limitation; 
broader global drug coverage is on the roadmap. 
• User Over-Reliance: Some users may treat MedLens summaries as a substitute for 
professional medical advice. Mitigated by prominent disclaimers on every screen and 
consistent 'consult your healthcare provider' messaging at key moments. 
• Interaction Checker Coverage: The interaction checker only surfaces interactions 
present in OpenFDA data. If data is unavailable, the app communicates this clearly 
rather than guessing. 
 
MVP Limitations 
• English only at launch. Additional global languages are planned for post-MVP releases. 
• Medicine cabinet stores drug name and simplified explanation only. Dosage tracking and 
scheduling are not included in MVP. 
• Interaction checker limited to data available in OpenFDA adverse event records. It is not 
a comprehensive clinical pharmacology tool.
